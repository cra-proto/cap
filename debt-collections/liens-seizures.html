Payments to the CRA

Putting a lien on or seizing your assets

In some cases when you have a debt you have not paid, we may use your personal belongings, or the assets of your business, to secure or pay your debt.

We may:

  • place a lien on your items to secure your outstanding debt
  • seize and sell your items to pay your outstanding debt

Before the CRA can place a lien on or seize your assets, we must get a provincial judgement or a certificate from the Federal Court legally certifying the amount you owe. This makes your debt a matter of public record.

Registering a lien on your assets

Does this section have enough information to apply to businesses as well as individuals?

Once your debt is legally certified, the CRA can register a lien against your assets and property, including your personal residence. Registering a lien will secure the amount of debt owing, by establishing creditor priority in the event of a sale. This means, if you sell your asset, your CRA tax debt is automatically paid from the proceeds of the sale, before you receive any remaining proceeds.

CBSA (customs) liens at the border

Once your debt is legally certified, the CRA may:

  • suspend your importation privileges
  • Apply a lien against goods imported by you
  • Apply a lien against goods reported for exportation by you

Seizing and selling your assets

Does this section have enough information to apply to businesses as well as individuals?

If your legally certified debt remains outstanding, the CRA may get a writ or memorial to seize and sell your assets and property. This could include your car, boat, artwork, cottage, rental property, or personal residence.

If the CRA sells your assets, the CRA will use the proceeds to pay:

  • your tax debt
  • any costs charged by the bailiff hired to sell the assets on behalf of the CRA

You will still have to pay any remaining tax debt.

Other legal collection actions we may take

Depending on your situation, there are different legal actions the CRA might take.

  • Garnishing your income and accounts
  • Making another person or entity co-liable for your debt

For more about how much time the CRA has to collect debts, refer to: How long a debt can be collected
