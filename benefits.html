Do your taxes to get payments

Doing your taxes is the only way to start and continue to receive benefit, credit and tax refund payments from the CRA. The information from your tax return is used to calculate your benefits and credits.

You and your spouse or common-law partner have until April 30 each year to do your taxes. Even if you do not owe taxes, are tax-exempt or have no income, you should do your taxes to avoid missing out on payments. You can even get benefits and credits going back 10 years.

If you live in Quebec, you also need to file a provincial tax return each year with Revenu Québec.

For more information, find out when you must file a tax return and how to get ready to do your taxes.

Get payments

By doing your taxes, you may be eligible for some of the payments below. You can use the Child and family benefits calculator to determine how much your payments from CRA may be.

GST/HST Credit

Provides four tax-free payments per year. If eligible, single individuals can get up to $496 per year. A couple with two children could get up to $992 per year.

In addition: If you qualify for the GST/HST Credit, you automatically qualify for certain benefits that CRA administers on behalf of the provinces and territories.

Payments from the provinces and territories related to the GST/HST Credit

Based on where you live, you may also get the:

  • BC climate action tax credit
  • New Brunswick harmonized sales tax credit
  • Newfoundland and Labrador income supplement
  • Newfoundland and Labrador disability amount
  • Newfoundland and Labrador seniors' benefit
  • Northwest Territories cost of living offset
  • Nova Scotia affordable living tax credit
  • Nunavut carbon credit
  • Prince Edward Island sales tax credit
  • Saskatchewan low-income tax credit (SLITC)
  • Yukon Government carbon price rebate - Individuals

Canada child benefit (CCB)

Provides monthly tax-free payments. Eligible families can get up to $7,437 per year for each child under 6 and up to $6,275 per year, for each child aged 6 to 17.

In addition: If you qualify for CCB, you automatically qualify for certain benefits that CRA administers on behalf of the provinces and territories.

Payments from the provinces and territories related to the CCB

Based on where you live, you may also get the:

  • Alberta child and family benefit
  • BC family benefit
  • New Brunswick child tax benefit
  • Newfoundland and Labrador child benefit (and pre-natal infant nutrition supplement)
  • Northwest Territories child benefit
  • Nova Scotia child benefit
  • Nunavut child benefit
  • Ontario child benefit (related to the Canada child benefit)
  • Yukon child benefit

Canada workers benefit (CWB)

Provides those working and earning a low income with an amount to reduce tax owed and send a refund. If eligible, individuals can get up to $1,428, and a family can get up to $2,461. Persons with disabilities who are approved for the DTC may be eligible for the CWB disability supplement and get an additional amount up to $737.

Climate action incentive payment

Provides a quarterly payment for residents of Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba, Newfoundland and Labrador, Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, Ontario and Prince Edward Island to offset the cost of federal pollution pricing.

Child disability benefit (CDB)

Provides up to $264.41 per child per month to families who care for a child under 18 who is eligible for the Disability Tax Credit. To get the CDB, you must also be eligible for the Canada child benefit.

Canada Dental Benefit

Provides tax-free payments to lower dental costs for eligible families with children under 12 who do not have private dental insurance. Eligible families can get up to $650 per child, up to a maximum of two payments per child.

Excise gasoline tax refund

If you have a permanent mobility impairment and cannot safely use public transportation, you can ask for a refund of part of the federal excise tax on gasoline you buy.

Disability Tax Credit (DTC)

Get access to more payments through the DTC. If you think you may be eligible, you should apply. Eligibility for the DTC opens the door to other federal, provincial or territorial programs.

Claim credits and other allowances

You may qualify to claim credits and allowances on your taxes.

Disability Tax Credit (DTC)

A tax credit for people with severe and prolonged impairments, or their supporting family member. The DTC lowers the amount of income tax they may owe. Eligible individuals can claim up to $8,870, with an additional $5,174 for those under 18.

Apply any time using the fully digital application form or printed paper form. For instructions on filling out the applicant and medical practitioner sections, see how to apply.

If you think you may be eligible, you should apply. Eligibility for the DTC opens the door to other federal, provincial or territorial programs.

Federal, provincial or territorial programs related to the DTC
  • Canada caregiver credit
  • Canada workers benefit disability supplement
  • Child disability benefit
  • Home accessibility tax credit
  • Multigenerational home renovation tax credit
  • Registered disability savings plan

Canada caregiver credit

A non-refundable tax credit that may be available to you if you support a spouse, a common-law partner, or other qualifying individuals with a mental or physical impairment.

Disability supports deduction

A deduction you can claim on your tax return if you have an impairment in physical or mental functions and have paid for certain medical expenses.

Home accessibility tax credit

A non-refundable tax credit for eligible renovations to improve the accessibility of your home.

Home buyers’ amount

A non-refundable tax credit for the purchase of a home. You do not have to be a first-time home buyer to claim the credit if you, or the relative you acquired the home for, are eligible for the DTC.

Medical expenses tax credit

A non-refundable tax credit that you can use to reduce the tax that you paid or may have to pay if you paid for healthcare expenses.

Multigenerational home renovation tax credit

A refundable tax credit you may be able to claim on your tax return to assist with the cost of renovating an eligible dwelling to establish a self-contained secondary unit.

Refundable medical expense supplement

A refundable tax credit you may be able to claim on your tax return if you had a modest income.

Provincial and territorial credits

You may also be entitled to other credits in addition to your federal credits depending on where you live.

Charitable tax credits

Donations to a Canadian registered charity or another qualified donee may make you eligible for a tax credit.

Home Buyers’ Plan

A program that allows you to withdraw from your registered retirement savings plans (RRSPs) to buy or build a home that fits your needs or for a related person with a disability.

RRSP deductions

Deductible contributions to a registered retirement savings plan can reduce the amount of tax you owe.

Amounts transferred from your spouse or common-law partner

A non-refundable tax credit for the transfer of certain amounts from a spouse of common-law partner.

Claim your expenses

Claiming expenses on your income tax return allows you to lower your taxable income and potentially reduce the amount of income tax you owe.

Common expenses that persons with disabilities claim:

  • Attendant care expenses
  • Medical expenses
  • Home accessibility expenses
  • Education expenses
  • Child care expenses

For all deduction types you can claim on your tax return, refer to: All deductions, credits and expenses.
